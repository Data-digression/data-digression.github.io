## Hate speech analysis with DeepSeek

Natural language processing (NLP) is a field I have been interested and involved for a while now. There are many possible applications around it, social media messages analysis being a relevant field of study. Sentiment analysis around these messages focuses on classifying the emotional tone of the content generated by users.

As an exercise, I wanted to perform a small field-test with local LLMs and the viability of using such tools for hate speech analysis.

Why hate speech? Anyone doomscrolling through nowadays social media might have noticed the roughness on some of the discussions. Studying the human (or bot) interactions is an interesting field for research. Also, the development of automated recognition tools is an effective usage of AI-based tools, where a human classification of messages would be unfeasable to scale.

---

### Hate Speech

#### Definitions

According to the United Nations, [hate speech](https://www.un.org/en/hate-speech/understanding-hate-speech/what-is-hate-speech) is  “any kind of communication in speech, writing or behaviour, that attacks or uses pejorative or discriminatory language with reference to a person or a group on the basis of who they are, in other words, based on their religion, ethnicity, nationality, race, colour, descent, gender or other identity factor.”

The definition is relatively vague, and can be subject to the interpretation of the person evaluating the communication. This is extremely relevant. How can we ask a machine learning (ML) model, or a large language model (LLM) to perform a valid evaluation when human annotators might evaluate them differently based on their personal values, judgement or other background specifics?


#### Available research on the topic

Other researchers have pointed out the particular inconsistencies of LLMs when thrown to this task[^1]. 

Without deep diving in a full state-of-the-art analysis (not intended by this humble blog post), I came accross an interesting study[^2] which publishes an interesting dataset which was referenced in previous issues[^3][^4]. The dataset contains 39565 comments annotated by 7912 annotators, and automated classifications of messages that can be considered hate speech.



### Getting things done

#### The setup

One of the main focuses of this test was to proof if a local instance of an open source model. Yes, data sovereignity, autonomy, you name it. But using a closed source SaaS LLM was out of the picture.

I tested the scripts with my desktop GPU which has 12GB of VRAM. This allowed me running deepseek-r1 up to the 14b model. For practical reasons (such as inference time) I ended up using the 8b and 1.5b models. The 1.5b would be extremely optimal. It can even run on a [Raspberry Pi](https://dev.to/jeremycmorgan/running-deepseek-r1-locally-on-a-raspberry-pi-1gh8) and other cheap hardware that would be awesome for researching on a home lab. More on the results with that later.

I went with a vast.ai instance with 12GB graphics card for the 135,556 combined annotated messages. The only reason for using a cloud service was not to have a desktop computer burning for three weeks.

In all cases the models where run in a local ollama instance.

#### The test(s)

The basic test included doing a query to DeepSeek with the evaluated messages. I ran the 1.5b and the 8b models with the aim of seeing how they behave.

The [script](https://github.com/Data-digression/deepseek-hate-speech-analysis/blob/main/hate-speech-testing.py) iterates through all the messages annotated from the Berkeley DLAB project and asks the DeepSeek models running on Ollama with the following prompt:

```python
system_prompt = (
        "You are a strict content-moderation system. "
        "Your task is to decide if the user's message is hate speech.\n\n"
        "Definition of hate speech: any attack, insult, threat, dehumanization, "
        "or incitement of discrimination or violence against a person or group "
        "based on protected characteristics such as race, ethnicity, nationality, "
        "religion, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, disability, "
        "migration status, or similar.\n\n"
        "Borderline cases (e.g. quoting slurs to condemn them) must not be marked "
        "as hate speech.\n\n"
        "Respond strictly in JSON and only the JSON object without the <think> section with the following keys:\n"
        "- is_hate_speech: boolean\n"
        "- severity: one of ['none', 'low', 'medium', 'high']\n"
        "- categories: array of strings (e.g. ['racism', 'sexism'])\n"
        "- explanation: short string\n" \
    )
```

This is the core logic of this exercise. Going into detail, we are assigning the model a persona, stating the task via the prompt, and then giving a definition of the elements that would account for a hate speech message. On the last part there is a disclaimer telling it not to consider borderline cases a positive, and a an output format specification.

Because some of the results that are showcased in the next section, I also used two alternative prompts ([this version](https://github.com/Data-digression/deepseek-hate-speech-analysis/blob/main/hate-speech-testing-prompt2.py) and [this other one](https://github.com/Data-digression/deepseek-hate-speech-analysis/blob/main/hate-speech-testing-prompt3.py)). The idea was to progressively be less strict with the specification of hate speech to the models. For these models I used sampling of 10000 rows just for the sake of not waiting for three weeks on each one.

A quick glimpse to the confusion matrixes are informative enough, and the numbers don't lie. The 1.5b model failed to provide relevant results, so I will focus on the 8b one. For some reason I didn't dig into, the 1.5b model also failed to evaluate many of the messages (hence the n=111272).
According to the results, DeepSeek-R1 8b (with the aforementioned prompt) did identify many more messages that were considered hate speech than the manual annotators, creating a vast amount of false positives. Therefore the precision is really low (0.5221). The high sensitivity of DeepSeek-R1 8b (0.9668) can be explained because it was able to detect almost all the relevant hate speech messages. However, it did retrieve many that the original dataset predictions did not find hateful enough.

The last point is interesting. I had the patience to evaluate some of the messages behind the scenes that were catalogued as [false positives](https://github.com/Data-digression/deepseek-hate-speech-analysis/blob/main/false_positives_deepseek8b.csv) in this test. The false positive file contains the message_id, the actual message, the scoring predicted on the original dataset and the results after evaluating the prompt by DeepSeek-R1 in 1.5b and 8b. It also contains the explanation on "why was this considered hate speech on the LLM evaluation". I did not look at the all the false positives listed on this file, but many of them are absolute examples of hate speech messages *according to the criteria used on this exercise*. 

And hence the major issue I found on this topic. What is hate speech?


Coming back to the alternative prompts tested, as it can be seen in the [notebook](https://github.com/Data-digression/deepseek-hate-speech-analysis/blob/main/testing-deepseek-hate-speech.ipynb), they didn't bring significative changes, although, the least specific the prompt is, the more similar to the original dataset predictions we appear to get (lowering the amount of false positives). However, those prompts have not been tested thoroughly enough to validate this as a definitive conclusion.


### Conclusions

A positive remark on DeepSeek-R1 with our prompt after evaluating the results is that we can state that it doesn't suffer from false negatives. The sensitivity is high, retrieving a very high proportion of the relevant messages.

The downside is that the precision is very low. The reason for this being that it triggers a lot of false positives. 

After an analysis on the false positives in the [file](https://github.com/Data-digression/deepseek-hate-speech-analysis/blob/main/false_positives_deepseek8b.csv) I'd say that alternative datasets shall be explored before drawing further conclusions.
